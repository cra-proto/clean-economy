What projects qualify

To qualify, in addition to other limitations, clean electricity property:

  • Must be equipment that is situated in and intended for use exclusively in Canada
  • Must substantially comply with the requirements of any environmental law, by-law, or regulation of Canada, a province, a municipality, or a municipal public body performing a function of government in Canada that are applicable to the property
  • Must not have been previously used, or acquired for use or lease, for any purpose before acquisition by the qualifying entity
  • If you lease the clean electricity property to another person or partnership

    If you lease the property to another person or partnership, additional leasing requirements must be met.

    The property must be both:

    • Leased to a qualifying entity or a partnership where all the members are qualifying entities
    • Leased in the ordinary course of carrying on a business in Canada by the qualifying entity whose principal business is any or a combination of the following:
      • Selling or servicing property of that type
      • Leasing property
      • Lending money
      • Purchasing conditional sales contracts, accounts receivable, bills of sale or chattel mortgages or hypothecary claims on movables, bills of exchange or other obligations representing all or part of the sale price of merchandise or services

What is eligible clean electricity property

The Clean Electricity ITC is available for investments in the following types of clean electricity property:

  • Qualified natural gas energy equipment
  • Certain equipment that is part of a hydro-electric installation, including a hydro-electric installation of any rated capacity
  • Equipment used to generate electricity from solar, wind, and water energy (excluding a test wind turbine as described under subsection 1219(3) of the Income Tax Regulations)
  • Concentrated solar energy equipment that is part of a system used solely for the purpose of generating electrical energy, exclusively from concentrated sunlight
  • Nuclear energy property
  • Geothermal equipment that is:
    • Used exclusively for the purpose of generating electrical energy, or both electrical energy and heat energy, solely from geothermal energy
    • Part of a system that exports more electrical energy than heat energy on a net annual basis, but excluding systems that extract fossil fuels for sale
    • Described in subparagraph (d)(vii) of Class 43.1 in the Income Tax Regulations
  • Waste biomass electricity generation equipment that is part of a system that exports more electrical energy than heat energy on a net annual basis
  • Fixed location energy storage property or pumped hydroelectric energy storage property, excluding equipment that uses any fossil fuel in operation
  • Qualified interprovincial transmission equipment
  • Property that is incorporated into another clean electricity property as part of a refurbishment, where the other property continues to be clean electricity property
  • Property used solely to convert another property that would not otherwise be described above if the conversion causes the other property to satisfy the description of equipment above

The following properties are excluded for the purposes of the Clean Electricity ITC:

  • Property included in Class 57 or 58 of Schedule II of the Income Tax Regulations
  • Equipment used for the off-site transmission, transportation or distribution of electricity or ammonia
  • Equipment used to prepare electricity for transport, including liquefaction equipment and equipment used to compress electricity to levels suitable for transportation
  • An automotive vehicle or related refuelling or charging equipment
  • A building or other structure
  • Construction equipment, furniture or office equipment
  • Equipment used for off-site storage

Determination of capital cost

The capital cost of property generally means your cost of acquiring the property and includes:

  • Legal, accounting, engineering or other fees incurred to acquire the property
  • Site preparation, delivery, installation, testing, or other costs incurred to put the property into service
  • In the case of a property that you manufacture for your own use, material, labour and overhead costs reasonably attributable to the property, but not any profit which might have been earned had the asset been sold

The capital cost of eligible clean electricity property must be reduced by:

  • An amount of any government assistance or non-government assistance you received in or before the tax year in which the property became available for use
  • An amount you are entitled to or can reasonably be expected to receive and that would be government assistance or non-government assistance if you received it in the year

Amounts of assistance that are repaid in a tax year or are no longer expected to be received in the year may be added to the capital cost of eligible clean electricity property for determining the Clean Electricity ITC for the year.

The cost of certain property that is used in both electricity and ammonia production will be allocated separately between the categories for such equipment.

The capital cost of eligible clean electricity property does not include:

  • Expenditures incurred for a preliminary clean electricity work activity, meaning work that is preliminary to the acquisition, construction, fabrication or installation of eligible clean electricity property, including but not limited to, a preliminary activity that is:
    • Obtaining permits or regulatory approvals
    • Performing front-end design or engineering work, including front-end engineering design studies (or equivalent studies as determined by the Minister of Natural Resources) but excluding detailed design or engineering work in relation to eligible clean electricity property
    • Conducting feasibility studies or pre-feasibility studies (or equivalent studies as determined by the Minister of Natural Resources)
    • Conducting environmental assessments
    • Clearing or excavating land
  • A portion of the capital cost of certain eligible clean electricity property that is not used all or substantially all to support a qualified clean electricity project
    •  In general, the included portion is the proportion of the output from the equipment that is expected to be used in the project relative to the total output from the equipment, with each determined over the first 20 years of the project’s operations

Refer to: Technical and Equipment Guidance – Clean Electricity ITC

If any part of the capital cost of a taxpayer’s eligible clean electricity property is unpaid on the day that is 180 days after the end of the taxation year of a taxpayer in which the property became available for use, that part of the cost is added to the capital cost of the property at the time it is paid.

Qualified natural gas energy equipment

Qualified natural gas energy equipment requires a system evaluation be completed by NRCan and has specific emissions intensity reporting requirements that are not applicable to other types of clean electricity property.

Learn more:

  • Submitting your qualified natural gas energy system plan</li>
  • Your reporting responsibilities

Qualified natural gas energy equipment refers to specific types of property that meet certain conditions:

  • It must be part of a system that:
    • Is fuelled all or substantially all by combustion of natural gas as determined on an annual basis and is not fuelled by anything other than the combustion of gaseous fuels
    • Is used to generate either electricity alone or both electricity and heat, determined without reference to carbon dioxide capture/li>
    • Exports more electricity than heat on a net basis as determined on an annual basis
    • Is physically and functionally integrated with equipment that captures and prepares or compresses carbon dioxide for transport
    • Uses less than 50% of its gross generated electricity to power the carbon dioxide capture equipment, as determined on an annual basis
    • Is expected to emit no more than 65 tonnes of carbon dioxide per gigawatt hour of gross electricity generated
    • Has been officially evaluated and approved by the Minister of Natural Resources
  • The equipment can include:
    • Electrical generating equipment
    • Heat-generating equipment used primarily to produce heat energy to operate the electrical generating equipment
    • Equipment that produces both electricity and heat
    • Equipment used solely for capturing, carbon dioxide generated by the system or for preparing, or compressing for transport carbon dioxide captured by the system
    • Heat recovery equipment used primarily for saving energy by reusing waste heat
    • Equipment for the distribution of heat within the system
    • Ancillary equipment that is physically and functionally integrated with and used solely to support the functioning of equipment described above
  • If the equipment was acquired before the system began operating, it must be verified by the Minister of Natural Resources
  • Some types of equipment do not qualify, including:
    • Buildings and structures
    • Transmission and distribution equipment
    • Equipment used to export heat energy from the system
    • Fuel storage and fuel handling equipment
    • Pollution abatement equipment

Concentrated solar energy equipment

Concentrated solar energy equipment refers to devices used primarily to generate heat, electricity, or both by focusing sunlight.

It includes:

  • Reflectors and tracking systems that follow the sun
  • Thermal receivers that capture heat
  • Storage equipment for thermal energy
  • Electric generators that produce electricity
  • Heat transfer systems that move heat
  • Electrical storage equipment to hold energy
  • Transmission equipment that carries electricity
  • Heat distribution equipment that delivers heat energy
  • Supporting structures built solely to house or hold solar equipment
  • Instrumentation and controls like weather monitoring tools

Nuclear energy property

Nuclear energy property refers to equipment used all or substantially all to generate electricity, or both electricity and heat, through nuclear fission.

This determination is made annually. The equipment must be part of a system that exports more electrical energy than heat energy on a net annual basis.

This includes:

  • Reactors and their components, such as vessels and control rods
  • Moderators that help manage nuclear reactions
  • Cooling systems that prevent overheating
  • Control systems that regulate operations
  • Fuel handling equipment for nuclear materials
  • Containment structures for safety
  • Electricity generators that produce power
  • Heat distribution equipment that delivers heat energy

Nuclear energy property does not include:

  • Nuclear fission fuel
  • Waste disposal equipment and sites
  • Transmission and distribution equipment
  • Properties included in Class 10 in the Income Tax Regulations
  • Certain properties listed in Class 17 in the Income Tax Regulations (except properties described under paragraph (a.1) of Class 17)

Waste biomass electricity generation equipment

Waste biomass electricity generation equipment refers to equipment that is part of a system that:

  • Operates solely to produce electricity or a mix of electricity and heat
  • Consumes material that derives all or substantially all its energy content from specified waste materials, as determined on an annual basis
  • Is located on a single site or adjacent sites functioning as one single integrated site
  • Does not exceed a heat rate of 11,000 BTU per kilowatt-hour, as calculated by a formula using energy output and fuel consumption on an annual basis

This includes:

  • Electrical generating equipment
  • Heat generators used primarily for producing heat energy to operate electrical generating equipment
  • Dual-purpose equipment producing both electricity and heat
  • Heat recovery equipment that reuses thermal waste
  • Biofuel production equipment that turns waste into fuel
  • Equipment to improve fuel combustion
  • Ancillary equipment that is physically and functionally integrated with the above equipment and that is used solely to support the functioning of the above equipment

This does not include:

  • Buildings and structures
  • Heat rejection equipment (such as a condenser and a cooling water system)
  • Transmission and distribution equipment
  • Equipment used to export heat, except specific ancillary supporting equipment
  • Fuel handling and storage equipment
  • Properties listed under Classes 57 and 58 in the Income Tax Regulations

Qualified interprovincial transmission equipment

It includes:

  • High-voltage transmission equipment, including cables and switches rated for at least 69 kilovolts
  • Electrical transmission structures, including towers and lattice frameworks
  • Related management equipment, including transformers, electric power conditioning equipment, and control equipment that are directly connected to the transmission infrastructure

It does not include:

  • Buildings
  • Distribution equipment, which typically delivers electricity to end-users

Refurbishment

Refurbishment refers to major changes or upgrades, such as significant alterations, renovations, improvements, or additions to a property that substantially:

  • Extend its useful life
  • Increase its capacity
  • Improve its efficiency
